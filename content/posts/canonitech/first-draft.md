## How it started

While I was working on the design team at Canonical, I wrote and presented a workshop on how to understand and use Git at a very basic level. The workshop went so well, I was asked to do more workshops on an ongoing basis.

It turns out that out of the technical employee training resources at Canonical, there wasn't anything inteded for non-engineers. A lot of the designers at the company felt like they didn't understand the most of the products our company made, and so there was a real appetite for entry level explanations of tech concepts.

I was given a team of 3 people and four months to run four workshops.

## Making a plan

When I started planning out the programming for "Canonitech", as the workshops were called, there were several guiding principles I was determined to keep us focused on.

### Entry-level focus 

As much as I would love to dive deeper in to the Git topic that I started with, I felt that it was important for each workshop to cover the 101-level of the topic it was on.

### Visuals-centred presentation

As a general rule, I believe that when you're explaining how something works, you should always start with a picture. All Canonitech workshop outlines start with a set of diagrams and illustrations that do the work of explaining the topic, rather than starting with a script.

### Naïveté-driven explanation

This is my general philosophy when it comes to explaining some kind of complicated system.

1. You start by explaining the problem that exists.
2. You imagine the most naïve solution to that problem
3. You identify the shortcomings and new problems introduced by that solution
4. You solve those secondary problems with further solutions.
5. After several iterations, you arrive at an approximation of the concept being explained.

The four workshops we ended up running were:

- Contain Multitudes: An introduction to containers and virtual machines for absolute beginners
- Major Development: An introduction to software development and operations for absolute beginners
- Byte Sized: An introduction to storage and filesystems for absolute beginners
- Net Positive: An introduction to networking for absolute beginners

All my workshops had some kind of silly pun as their short form title, and this was for a very specific reason.

All companies generate a huge volume of internal and external content, and eventually it becomes hard to keep track of. This can make it difficult to communicate about specific assets with people in a way where you both know which exact thing you're talking about, without sending them the Google Drive link.

I knew that the purpose of these workshops was not just as a learning opportunity for the people there, but also to be an ongoing training resource for people who joined the team later. I knew that if my lectures had generic titles, it would be difficult for new people to track them down or know they were looking at exact video they were told to watch.

## How it went

The workshops were presented roughly one per month, and they were very well-attended. Almost the entire design team of about 30 designers would join every session.

I was always very encouraged by the level of anticipation to our workshops when we announced each upcoming one, as well as the level of engagement and insightful questions we would get during the workshop itself.
